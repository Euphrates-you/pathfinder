FOUR IDEAS THAT
RUN THE COSMOS

Nearly everything in astrophysics — and every rocket ever flown — comes back to these four.

A.01

GRAVITY

F = G·m₁m₂ / r²

Every mass pulls on every other mass. It’s the weakest force by far, but it never cancels out and never quits — so at cosmic scales it wins everything: it shapes orbits, ignites stars, and sculpts galaxies.

A.02

LIGHT

c = 299,792 km/s

Light is the only messenger the universe sends us, and it obeys a hard speed limit. That limit turns telescopes into time machines: look far enough away and you are looking billions of years into the past.

A.03

FUSION

E = mc²

Squeeze hydrogen hard enough and it fuses into helium, converting a sliver of mass into a torrent of energy. That’s what a star is: a fusion reactor held together by its own gravity.

A.04

SPACETIME

gravity = curvature

Einstein’s upgrade to Newton: mass doesn’t “pull,” it bends the fabric of space and time itself, and things simply follow the curves. GPS satellites must correct for it every single day.

( 04 )

BLACK HOLES:
WHERE GRAVITY WINS

Cram enough mass into a small enough space and escape velocity exceeds the speed of light. Since nothing outruns light, nothing gets out. The point of no return is the event horizon.

r_s = 2GM / c²

The Schwarzschild radius — how small an object must be squeezed to become a black hole. For the Sun: about 3 km. For Earth: the size of a grape.

TIME SLOWS NEAR THEM

Deep in a black hole’s gravity well, clocks genuinely tick slower relative to distant observers — not a trick of perception, but real relativistic time dilation.

SPAGHETTIFICATION IS REAL

Near a small black hole, gravity on your feet vastly exceeds gravity on your head. You’d be stretched into a noodle of atoms. (Astronomers really do call it that.)

ONE ANCHORS OUR GALAXY

SGR A* — 4 million solar masses — sits at the center of the Milky Way. We’ve photographed its shadow. Almost every large galaxy hosts one.

( 06 )

THE STORY OF
EVERYTHING

13.8 billion years ago, all of space — not stuff in space, space itself — began expanding from an unimaginably hot, dense state. We can trace the story back to a trillionth of a trillionth of a second.

T = 10⁻⁴³ S

THE PLANCK ERA

The earliest moment physics can describe. Temperature: 10³² °C. All four forces possibly unified. Our equations break down before this point — the honest answer is “we don’t know yet.”

T = 10⁻³⁶ S

INFLATION

Space itself expands by a factor of at least 10²⁶ in a fraction of a second — faster than light (space can do that; things in space can’t). Quantum flickers get stretched into the seeds of future galaxies.

T = 3 MIN

FIRST NUCLEI

The universe cools enough for protons and neutrons to stick together. In about 17 minutes it forges roughly 75% hydrogen and 25% helium — the recipe stars still use today.

T = 380,000 YR

FIRST LIGHT

Atoms finally form, and light can travel freely for the first time. That flash still fills the sky today as the cosmic microwave background — a baby picture of the universe we can actually photograph.

T ≈ 200 MYR

FIRST STARS IGNITE

Gravity pulls gas into clumps until fusion sparks. The cosmic dark ages end. These first giants live fast, die as supernovae, and seed space with the first heavy elements.

T ≈ 9.2 BYR

THE SUN FORMS

In a quiet arm of an unremarkable spiral galaxy, a cloud of recycled star-debris collapses. Leftover crumbs become planets. One crumb ends up at exactly the right distance for liquid water.

T = 13.8 BYR

NOW

A species of curious primates works out the entire story above using math, telescopes, and about a century of effort — then starts building machines to go see it in person.

( 07 )

THROW MASS ONE WAY,
GO THE OTHER

That’s it. That’s the whole trick. A rocket doesn’t push against air or the launch pad — it hurls tons of gas out the back at enormous speed, and Newton’s third law pushes the rocket forward in exchange. It works in a vacuum better than in air.

F = ṁ · vₑ

Thrust = mass flow rate × exhaust velocity. Want more push? Throw more mass per second, or throw it faster. Every engine ever designed is a different answer to that trade-off.

TWR > 1

Thrust-to-weight ratio. To leave the pad, thrust must beat weight. A Falcon 9 lifts off around TWR ≈ 1.3 — nine engines producing the power of about five 747s at full throttle.

WHY IT WORKS IN A VACUUM

The rocket pushes on its own exhaust, and the exhaust pushes back — no air required. In space there’s also no drag and no pressure fighting the nozzle, so engines actually perform better up there.

THE TYRANNY OF THE
ROCKET EQUATION

Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, 1903: a rocket’s total change in velocity (Δv, “delta-v”) depends only on exhaust speed and the ratio of full-to-empty mass. The cruel part is the logarithm — fuel has to lift fuel, so doubling your fuel does not double your Δv. Try to reach orbit:

Δv = vₑ · ln( m₀ / m_f )

THE CHEAT CODE: STAGING

A single rocket struggles to reach a mass ratio of ~10 (tanks and engines weigh something). The fix: once a tank is empty, throw it away. Each stage starts the equation fresh, without hauling dead weight.

STEP 1

Full stack lifts off. The huge first stage does the brutal work of climbing out of thick air and deep gravity.

STEP 2

First stage burns out and separates — tons of dead tank gone in an instant. (Some now fly home and land themselves.)

STEP 3

The lighter second stage ignites and finishes the job. Same fuel budget, far more Δv — enough for orbit.

THREE WAYS
TO THROW MASS

Every engine trades thrust against efficiency (specific impulse, Isp — roughly “miles per gallon” for rockets). Nothing does both well. Yet.

E.01

SOLID FUEL

A tube packed with rubbery explosive. Gloriously simple, storable for years, huge thrust — but once lit, it cannot be throttled or shut off. Used for boosters and missiles.

  • ISP~250 S
  • THRUST█████very high

E.02

LIQUID FUEL

Pumps feed fuel and liquid oxygen into a combustion chamber. Fiendishly complex — but throttleable, restartable, and efficient. The workhorse of every orbital rocket flying today.

  • ISP300–450 S
  • THRUST████high
  • CONTROL█████very high
E.03

ION DRIVE

Electric fields fling charged xenon atoms out at 30+ km/s. Thrust of a sheet of paper resting on your hand — but sustained for years, it outruns any chemical rocket. Deep-space specialist.

A REAL FLIGHT TO ORBIT, IN SIX MOVES

  1. T+0:00
    LIFTOFF. Engines throttle up, TWR crosses 1, and half a million kilograms starts moving upward with surprising slowness.
  2. T+1:10
    MAX-Q. Peak aerodynamic pressure — the atmosphere pushes back hardest here, so engines briefly throttle down. The vehicle is already tilting: the gravity turn.
  3. T+2:30
    MECO & STAGING. Main engines cut off; the empty first stage separates. Explosive bolts, cold-gas pushers, and physics do the rest.
  4. T+3:00
    FAIRING JETTISON. Above the atmosphere, the payload no longer needs its shell. Every kilogram dropped is Δv gained.
  5. T+8:30
    ORBITAL INSERTION. The second stage pushes horizontal speed to ~7.8 km/s. Engines cut off — and everything aboard is now falling around the Earth, weightless.
  6. LATER
    GOING FURTHER. More Δv stretches the orbit to the Moon or beyond — often stealing momentum from planets themselves via gravity assists. Voyager 1 has been coasting on those since 1977.
